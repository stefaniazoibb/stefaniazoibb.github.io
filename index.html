I work on machine learning for vision — both the biological kind and the artificial kind. My PhD is part of BE-LIGHT, a Marie Skłodowska-Curie network on light-based biomedical diagnostics, and my piece of it is the optical decomposition of the retina: what the retina does to incoming light, and how to model that well enough to be useful in a diagnostic setting.

Most of my modelling is probabilistic. I use Gaussian processes a lot — on natural images and video, on neural and physiological recordings — mainly because I want a posterior rather than a point estimate. If a model is going to inform a clinical decision, "how sure are you" is not a footnote. The other lens I keep coming back to is information theory: entropy and mutual information are a fairly honest way to ask what a signal actually carries, and efficient coding says the retina is compressing the world into as few bits as it can get away with. Which is, more or less, what we ask representation learning to do too.

I came to this sideways. Bachelor's in psychology and neuropsychology in Thessaloniki, then a master's in biomedical engineering in Paris, and only then the computational side. That route cost me some time and gave me a fair amount of breadth, and on balance I'd take the trade again.

Research

  • Gaussian processes and Bayesian inference. Kernel design for spatiotemporal structure, hyperparameters by marginal likelihood, calibration and uncertainty on noisy multi-modal data.
  • Retinal coding. Spike-triggered analysis, receptive-field estimation, and how retinal circuits turn light into spikes.
  • Representation learning. Self-supervised vision encoders, and what they actually learn — shape versus texture, and how that shows up in out-of-distribution behaviour.
  • Efficient coding and information theory. Efficient-coding models of the retina; mutual information between stimulus and response; ordinal-pattern and permutation-entropy methods for complexity in neural time series; excitability models and bifurcations.

Selected projects

Optical decomposition of the retina

The core of my PhD. Computational and ML models of how the retina encodes light, read through the lens of efficient coding — treating the retina as a system that compresses the visual world into as few bits as it can — in service of light-based diagnostics. Ongoing.

Shape vs. texture bias in self-supervised encoders

Linear probes on frozen CLAPP representations with cue-conflict stimuli, to measure whether the features a model builds are shape-driven or texture-driven, and how the training schedule moves that. Texture bias is the boring answer and it keeps winning, which is the interesting part.

Gaussian processes on natural images and video

GP priors whose covariance encodes spatial and temporal structure, conditioned on data, with the usual marginal-likelihood tuning. Mostly NumPy and SciPy. Good for building intuition about what a kernel is really assuming.

Bayesian model of pilot physiological state

Done at Thales AVS: forecasting how quickly pilots return to physiological baseline, fusing eye tracking, ECG, respiration and accelerometer data recorded during flight simulation. The Bayesian treatment was the point — a confident wrong answer is worse than a wide interval.
